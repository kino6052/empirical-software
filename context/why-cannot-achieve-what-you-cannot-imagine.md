"If you can't imagine it, you can't get it done."

This simple statement is one of the most underrated laws of productivity and success. Yet, most of modern professional life is built to violate it. We have replaced tangible action with abstract language, lofty titles, and financial jargon that sounds impressive but explains nothing.

If you want to understand why some people execute flawlessly while others merely "strategize," you need to understand one thing: grounding. It is the most important requirement for real-world results. Without it, you are not managing a business—you are reciting a spell you don't understand.

## The Disease of Abstraction

Ask someone what they do for a living. If they reply with a title like "Chief Revenue Officer of a cyber-security firm" or "Senior Volatility Strategist," you still have no idea what they actually do. You don't know how they start their day, what problem they solve, or why anyone pays them.

Businesspeople are the worst offenders. They deal in reifications—turning actions into abstract nouns. Interest. Volatility. Liquidity. EBITDA. These words are useful for reports, but they are deadly for execution. They are ghosts. You cannot touch volatility. You cannot sit down and "do" liquidity. Yet entire careers are built on shuffling these ghosts around.

The problem is not the existence of abstractions. The problem is mistaking them for reality.

## The Grounding Question

Here is the simple test that separates fluff from function. Instead of asking for a title, ask this:

"What did you actually do on your first day in that role?"

Not your responsibilities. Not your mission statement. Your actions. Your concrete, empirical, observable steps.

- Did you pick up a phone and read a script?
- Did you open a spreadsheet and subtract one cell from another?
- Did you walk to a whiteboard and draw a decision tree?
- Did you approve or reject a specific invoice?

Suddenly, the fog clears. You realize that a "CFO in cyber-security" does not "manage capital allocation" in the abstract. They spend their day in meetings. And in those meetings, they do exactly three concrete things: listen, compare numbers, and say "yes" or "no" to a request for $50,000 in new servers.

Why can't a regular person make that decision? Because the CFO has seen 200 previous server requests and knows which ones led to waste. That is real. That is grounded. But the title alone tells you none of this.

## Let's Ground the Common Roles

Take every modern profession and strip away the abstraction. See what remains:

- A salesperson does not "drive revenue" or "manage the funnel." They sit in a cubicle, dial a phone, and read a 3-minute script. If the prospect says a specific keyword, they move to page 2 of the script. That is the job.
- A CEO does not "set vision." Their day is a sequence of interruptions. They walk into a room, listen to a product manager argue for two more weeks of development, and decide "yes" or "no." Then they walk into the next room and repeat.
- A business owner does not "build equity." They check their bank balance at 8 AM. If it is below payroll, they call three clients and ask for an early payment. If it is above, they call one client and take a nap.
- A consultant does not "deliver transformation." They open a slide deck they inherited from last year, change the client's logo, and rehearse a story where the problem is always "people" and the solution is always "process."

This sounds cynical. It is not. It is liberating. Because once you see the concrete action, you can improve it. You cannot improve "EBITDA." You can improve the 15-minute phone script.

## Why This Matters for Success

The original claim was: If you can't imagine it, you can't get it done. This is not a metaphor. It is a neurological fact. Your brain cannot execute a command it cannot picture. Try to picture "increasing stakeholder value." Now try to picture "opening your email and typing a 50-word reply to the Acme Corp invoice." Only the second one is real.

Every successful execution—every business built, every problem solved, every dollar earned—is a chain of concrete actions. Abstractions are just the labels we glue onto the chain afterward.

The people who actually get things done are not the ones with the most abstract vocabulary. They are the ones who, when asked "what do you do?", answer with verbs. They call. They write. They decide. They walk from one room to another.

## The Rebuttal (And Why It Fails)

Someone will argue: "But we need abstractions to coordinate! A CEO cannot describe every phone call. That's why we have terms like 'strategy' and 'liquidity.'"

Fair enough. Abstractions are useful shortcuts—after you have grounded them. The disaster happens when we teach the shortcut before the concrete reality. That is how you get a business graduate who can define "EBITDA" in three different ways but cannot tell you what a single employee actually builds with their hands.

Use the abstraction as a map. But never forget that the map is not the territory. The territory is the phone call, the spreadsheet cell, the nodded head in a meeting room.

## The Bottom Line

If you want to succeed—really succeed, not just sound important—do this exercise tomorrow morning. Write down your own job as a list of concrete actions, hour by hour. Then ask: If these actions are all I do, am I creating value? If you cannot answer, you are not grounded. And if you are not grounded, you are not executing. You are just attending meetings about meetings.

And nobody ever built anything real from a meeting about a meeting.
